DESIGN AND ANALYSIS OF ALGORITHMS
Health & Infrastructure – Business Case Studies for the City of “Prithvi Lok”
Region 150 sq km (Prithvi Lok Urban Region)
Budget ₹20,000 crore (Multi-year plan)
Theme Smart Health Infrastructure & City-wide Optimization
Focus Realistic use of advanced algorithms & data structures

The fictional city Prithvi Lok is a 150 sq km tier-2 urban region with mixed residential, industrial, and peri-urban zones. With a planned health-infrastructure budget of ₹20,000 crore, the city aims to reduce emergency response time, improve hospital connectivity, digitize health records, and ensure equitable access to services. Below are 10 realistic business case studies where advanced algorithms and data structures (Heap, Segment Tree, Fenwick Tree, Hash Tables, Trie, BST, Red-Black Tree, DFS/BFS, MST (Kruskal), Bellman–Ford, Dijkstra, Floyd) are applied to solve real-world problems.

Business Case Studies – Prithvi Lok

CASE 01

Emergency Ambulance Routing & Coverage

Problem / Business Case: Reduce average ambulance response time across Prithvi Lok from 15 minutes to under 8 minutes, considering traffic congestion and multiple hospital choices.

Algorithms: Dijkstra’s Algorithm

Data Structures: Min-Heap (priority queue), adjacency list representation of road graph

Purpose: Compute fastest time routes from ambulance hubs to all key localities and precompute “nearest hospital by travel time” for each ward.

Efficiency: Time: O(E log V) for each run; with precomputation from hub locations, routing becomes real-time for emergencies.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3 (Good Health), SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities) – Response time reduced by ~45%, saving critical-care patients and improving trust in public health services.

CASE 02

City-Wide Oxygen & Utility Network Planning

Problem / Business Case: Design a cost-effective pipeline and utility network connecting oxygen plants to major hospitals and COVID-ready centers within the 150 sq km area.

Algorithms: Minimum Spanning Tree (Kruskal’s)

Data Structures: Disjoint Set (for Kruskal’s), edge list, Red-Black Tree (for maintaining sorted edges / cost buckets)

Purpose: Build a minimum cost connection network ensuring all critical facilities are connected to oxygen supply with redundancy where needed.

Efficiency: Time: O(E log E); reduces redundant pipeline length and construction cost.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3, SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation) – Infrastructure cost cut by ~20%, while ensuring uninterrupted supply to ICUs during peak demand.

CASE 03

Affordable Transit for Hospital Access

Problem / Business Case: Design a subsidy model for public transport routes that connect low-income neighborhoods to tertiary hospitals, where some routes have negative “cost” (subsidies).

Algorithms: Bellman–Ford

Data Structures: Edge list for bus corridors, Hash Table for storing fare rules and patient-category discounts

Purpose: Compute minimum travel cost including subsidies and ensure there are no harmful negative cycles in the pricing model.

Efficiency: Time: O(V · E). Run daily/weekly for pricing updates; acceptable for city-scale graph sizes.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 1 (No Poverty), SDG 3 – Travel cost to hospitals drops significantly for vulnerable groups, improving treatment adherence.

CASE 04

Real-Time Hospital Bed & ICU Capacity Dashboard

Problem / Business Case: Central command center must answer queries such as “ICU beds free in Zone-3 between 6–9 PM” quickly for 26+ hospitals.

Algorithms: Segment Tree, Fenwick Tree (Binary Indexed Tree)

Data Structures: Segment Tree over time slots and hospitals, Fenwick Tree for incremental updates

Purpose: Support fast range queries (sum / min capacity) and updates (admission or discharge) over time and geography.

Efficiency: Time: O(log N) per update and query, enabling near real-time dashboards even under heavy load.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3 – Better load balancing across hospitals, fewer rejections due to “No Bed” situation, optimal use of existing infrastructure.

CASE 05

Unified Smart Health Card & Record Lookup

Problem / Business Case: Prithvi Lok wants a single digital health ID for every citizen to access records from any hospital instantly and reduce duplicate registrations.

Algorithms: Hash-based lookup schemes

Data Structures: Hash Tables (for patient IDs → records), Red-Black Tree (for indexing by time / priority)

Purpose: Enable O(1) average-time search for patient profiles and maintain ordered views (by last visit, severity) using a balanced tree.

Efficiency: Hash Table: Average O(1) insert/search; Red-Black Tree: O(log N) ordered operations.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3, SDG 9 – Reduced duplicate records, faster registration, and more accurate patient history at point of care.

CASE 06

Medicine & Diagnostic Search in City e-Health Portal

Problem / Business Case: Citizens need to quickly find availability of medicines, lab tests, and departments across multiple hospitals using keyword or prefix search.

Algorithms: Trie-based prefix search

Data Structures: Trie (Prefix Tree), Hash Tables for mapping hospital IDs and pricing info

Purpose: Enable auto-complete search (“dia…”, “cardio…”) and instant mapping to facilities that offer those services.

Efficiency: Time: O(L) per query where L is length of search string, independent of number of entries.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3, SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities) – Better information access for all citizens, minimizing “information gap” between rich and poor neighborhoods.

CASE 07

Prioritization of Health Infrastructure Projects

Problem / Business Case: Select which hospitals, PHCs, and telemedicine centers to build first under the fixed ₹20,000 crore budget, considering benefit scores and urgency.

Algorithms: BST-based priority ordering, Red-Black Tree for balanced scheduling

Data Structures: Binary Search Tree / Red-Black Tree keyed by a composite score (coverage, cost, urgency)

Purpose: Maintain a dynamically changing priority list of projects as new data (population growth, disease hotspots) is added.

Efficiency: O(log N) insertion, deletion, and re-prioritization, supporting continuous planning cycles.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3, SDG 11 – Ensures that the most impactful projects are funded and executed first, maximizing health coverage per rupee spent.

CASE 08

Surgery & Diagnostic Scheduling in Major Hospitals

Problem / Business Case: Large hospitals in Prithvi Lok must schedule surgeries and diagnostics based on urgency, resource availability, and patient condition.

Algorithms: Heap-based priority scheduling

Data Structures: Binary Heap (min-heap / max-heap), possibly Fenwick Tree for tracking daily slot usage

Purpose: Always process the highest priority patient next while keeping utilization of operation theatres and labs high.

Efficiency: Insert / extract: O(log N). Supports frequent updates due to emergencies and cancellations.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3 – Reduced waiting time for critical cases, more predictable schedules for elective cases.

CASE 09

Disease Spread Analysis Across Wards

Problem / Business Case: Understand how a contagious disease (like dengue or flu) spreads between wards, slums, and peri-urban villages of Prithvi Lok.

Algorithms: DFS + BFS over contact and mobility graphs

Data Structures: Graph adjacency lists, Hash Tables for ward attributes and case counts

Purpose: Identify connected components, high-risk clusters, and shortest infection paths for targeted interventions like fogging or vaccination camps.

Efficiency: Time: O(V + E) per traversal; scalable for city-sized networks.

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3, SDG 11 – Early hotspot identification leads to targeted, cheaper, and more effective control strategies.

CASE 10

All-Pairs Travel Time Matrix for Health Facilities

Problem / Business Case: City planners need a matrix of travel times between all major hospitals, PHCs, and ambulance hubs for referral policies and capacity planning.

Algorithms: Floyd (Floyd–Warshall) All-Pairs Shortest Paths

Data Structures: 2D matrix of size N × N for facilities, Hash Table for mapping facility names to indices

Purpose: Precompute minimum travel time between every pair of facilities to design referral chains and diversion rules when some hospitals are overloaded.

Efficiency: Time: O(N³). Suitable because N (number of main facilities) is moderate (e.g., 30–50).

SDGs and Outcome: SDG 3, SDG 9 – Supports strategic planning of hub-and-spoke models, improving regional accessibility within the 150 sq km area.
